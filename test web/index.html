Efficient Single-GPU Performance Modeling for LLM Training Time Prediction Using RTX 4090 Abstract: Training Large Language Models (LLMs) has emerged as one of the most computationally intensive and resource-demanding processes in modern artificial intelligence. With models scaling to billions of parameters, accurately estimating training time is crucial for efficient resource allocation, cost optimization, and experimental planning. Existing approaches for predicting training time rely either on full-system simulation, which is computationally expensive, or partial execution, which incurs significant cost and time overhead. In this paper, we propose a comprehensive operator-level performance modeling framework for predicting end-to-end LLM training time on a single GPU, specifically the NVIDIA RTX 4090. Our approach decomposes the LLM computation graph into atomic operators, performs extensive micro-benchmarking across diverse configurations, and employs machine learning-based regression models to estimate operator runtime. By leveraging the repetitive structure of transformer architectures and simplifying distributed communication overhead into a single-GPU paradigm, we significantly reduce modeling complexity while maintaining high accuracy. We validate our framework using Qwen3.5-0.8B and demonstrate realistic use-case scenarios with varying token scales (10B–100B tokens). Our approach provides a scalable, efficient, and practical alternative to traditional methods, enabling accurate prediction without executing expensive training runs. Index Terms Large Language Models, Performance Modeling, GPU Computing, RTX 4090, Operator-Level Modeling, Machine Learning Systems I. Introduction The rapid evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) has fundamentally transformed artificial intelligence. However, training these models is extremely expensive in terms of both time and computational resources. Predicting training time beforehand is therefore critical for planning, budgeting, and efficient system utilization. Despite its importance, training time prediction remains challenging due to heterogeneous workloads, hardware dependency, and complex execution pipelines. Existing solutions such as simulation-based and sampling-based approaches are either too slow or too costly. This work proposes a novel operator-level modeling framework that eliminates the need for simulation or execution by learning runtime behavior directly from micro-benchmarks. Purpose: Motivate need for prediction by showing how expensive real training is. Recent empirical training runs on large-scale HPC systems further highlight the importance of accurate training-time prediction. For example, pretraining a 500M-parameter transformer model on 8× NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80GB GPUs requires over 136 days to process 100 billion tokens, even with optimized data-parallel execution. Such prolonged runtimes make trial-and-error configuration tuning infeasible, reinforcing the need for accurate, execution-free performance modeling. II. Problem Statement: The goal is to accurately predict end-to-end LLM training time without executing training. This is difficult due to heterogeneous operations, nonlinear hardware behavior, and limitations of existing methods. We reformulate the problem as a data-driven operator-level prediction task. III. Proposed Approach: We model training as a composition of atomic operators and use regression models to predict their runtime. Transformer repetition allows scalability. IV. Methodology: We formalize our approach as a structured pipeline consisting of six key stages: Step 1: Choose LLM Model We begin by selecting the target LLM architecture (e.g., Qwen3.5-0.8B). The model configuration includes parameters such as number of layers, hidden size, sequence length, and attention heads. Step 2: Break Model into Operators; The selected model is decomposed into atomic operators such as: * GEMM (matrix multiplications) * Attention (QKV projections, softmax) * Normalization (LayerNorm/RMSNorm) * Activation functions and so on This step converts the model into a structured operator graph. Operator Composition Reference from Real Training Runs Operator categories Relative flop contributions Layer repetition structure To ground our operator-level decomposition in realistic training behavior, we reference operator composition statistics extracted from a full pretraining run of a Qwen1.5–0.5B model executed on 8× NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80GB GPUs. The per-batch computational breakdown reveals that embedding operations account for approximately 48.4% of FLOPs, while attention and MLP blocks each contribute 23.5%. Normalization and activation functions jointly represent less than 5% of total computation. This empirical distribution closely matches the operator structure assumed in our modeling framework and further justifies focusing on GEMM-heavy kernels and attention primitives when building regression-based runtime predictors. Step 3: Micro-Benchmarking (Runtime Collection); Each operator is independently benchmarked on RTX 4090 across multiple configurations: * Batch size (b) * Sequence length (l) * Hidden dimension (d) * Attention heads (h) This produces a dataset mapping input configurations to execution time. Step 4: Build Regression Models; We train machine learning models such as: * Random Forest * XGBoost These models learn nonlinear relationships between operator parameters and runtime. Step 5: Combine Operator Predictions; Using the trained regressors, we predict runtime for each operator instance in the model. Since transformer layers repeat, predictions are scaled accordingly. Step 6: Predict Full Training Runtime; Finally, we aggregate all operator runtimes and add system overhead (GPU–CPU offload) to estimate total training time. V. Mathematical Formulation T_total = L × (Σ T_op) + T_offload Where L is number of layers and T_op represents operator runtime. VI. For prediction experiments, we target Qwen3.5–0.8B, which reflects a realistic mid-scale LLM suitable for single-GPU training studies. For empirical reference and validation, we analyze a Qwen1.5–0.5B pretraining run executed on large-scale HPC systems (Perlmutter and Vista). Both models share similar architectural characteristics: Decoder-only autoregressive transformer Multi-head self-attention Feed-forward (MLP) blocks Layer normalization and activation functions This architectural similarity allows meaningful operator-level comparison across hardware platforms. VIII. Results and Analysis A. Prediction Accuracy and Trends Our operator-level regression models accurately capture nonlinear runtime behavior across GEMM, attention, and normalization operators. Predicted runtimes closely follow expected scaling trends with respect to batch size, sequence length, and hidden dimension. Unlike analytical FLOP-based models, the learned regressors implicitly account for: Memory bandwidth effects Kernel launch overheads Hardware-specific scheduling behavior B. Operator Contribution Analysis Both predicted (RTX 4090) and empirical (A100) results consistently show that: Embedding and projection GEMMs dominate FLOPs Attention and MLP blocks contribute comparable cost Normalization and activation functions have negligible impact This alignment validates the operator selection and decomposition strategy used in our framework. C. Scalability and Token-Level Extrapolation Empirical results demonstrate linear scaling of training time with token count over long horizons (100B–500B tokens). This directly supports the mathematical formulation: T_"total" =L×∑T_"op" +T_"overhead" Because per-batch operator execution remains invariant, total runtime can be extrapolated reliably without full execution. D. Hardware Utilization and Bottlenecks Despite operating on A100 GPUs, measured utilization remains around 22%. This highlights that LLM training is frequently memory- and latency-bound, not purely compute-bound. Our operator-level approach is well-suited to capture these effects, as it models observed kernel runtimes rather than relying on peak hardware specifications E. Discussion The combination of predicted RTX 4090 runtimes and empirical A100 reference data demonstrates that operator-level modeling offers an effective balance between accuracy and efficiency. By avoiding simulation and partial execution, our framework enables rapid, low-cost training time estimation while remaining grounded in real-world behavior. Contrast measured GPU utilization vs predicted operator bounds. Interestingly, despite running on high-end A100 hardware, the measured GPU utilization remains low (~22%), suggesting that large-scale LLM training can be bottlenecked by memory access patterns and synchronization overheads rather than raw compute. This observation further motivates our operator-level modeling approach, which explicitly learns kernel-level performance behavior rather than relying solely on peak FLOP assumptions. IX.
